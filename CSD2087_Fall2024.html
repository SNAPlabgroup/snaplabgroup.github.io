Research Presentations

Welcome to the Research Presentations page for the Fall 2023 edition of CSD 2087 Hearing Conservation and Restoration at the Department of Communication Science and Disorders at University of Pittsburgh. Student presentations are showcased below with their permission.

Abstract: Noise is present in everyday life and can be classified as traumatic (such as a firework) or average and considerably safe (such as daily conversational exposure). The article review addresses is central damage occurs as a result of this non-traumatic noise exposure long-term. Clinically patients will audiometric normal hearing thresholds will report disproportional difficulties with speech perception and listening with competing noise which indicates subclinical auditory damage. These symptoms align closely with central auditory processing disorder (APD). Although there are tests for sound processing symptoms (temporal, binaural integration testing, speech in noise), there is not yet a measure of central function used clinically. In part, the lack of test development contributes to the ongoing research required to fully understand the mechanisms of central auditory damage. The articles reviewed in this presentation use animal and human data to support that central system damage does occur evident by tonotopic re-organization, overall degradation in neural synchrony, changes in spontaneous firing rates, and more. Frequency representation changes were represented in the midbrain, thalamus, and cortex.
